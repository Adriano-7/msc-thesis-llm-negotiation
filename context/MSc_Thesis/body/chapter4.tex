\chapter{Results and Discussion}

In this chapter, we benchmark the selected models on NegotiationArena (Section~\ref{sec:results-benchmark}), comparing families, tiers, and roles, and relating the observed patterns to those reported by \textcite{bianchi_how_2024}. We then investigate the effect of social personas (Section~\ref{sec:results-personas}) and discuss the impact of the proposed inference-time techniques: self-refine (Section~\ref{sec:results-self-refine}) and team-based negotiation (Section~\ref{sec:results-team}).
 
\section{Open-Weight Benchmark} \label{sec:results-benchmark}

\subsection{Completion and Parse-Error Self-Correction}

% WHY DO GAMES FAIL? WHO FAILS THE MOST?

Every game depends on the model's ability to follow the protocol and output the correct XML tags. A single missing tag can cause a game to fail prematurely, which is a problem for small open-weight models. Without self-correction, more than a quarter of BuySell games and nearly a fifth of Trading games end prematurely at the very-small tier (Figure~\ref{fig:parse-completion-retry-bars} , Appendix~\ref{ap3:additional-results}).


Table~\ref{tab:cross-play-completion} shows that completion without retries does not scale uniformly with model size. The small tier is the most reliable, with at least 95\% completion in all three games, while the medium tier falls to 67.8\% in Trading and 86.7\% in the Ultimatum Game.

Failures are also unevenly distributed across models (Figure~\ref{fig:parse-errors-by-model}): \texttt{Gemma-3-4B-it} alone accounts for 69 parse errors, followed by \texttt{Qwen-3.5-27B} (45) and \texttt{Mistral-Small-3.2-24B} (43). The main reasons for failure are game-specific (Figure~\ref{fig:parse-errors-by-game}, Appendix~\ref{ap3:additional-results}): in Trading, games end mainly due to a missing \texttt{<player answer>} tag or unparseable resource amounts; in BuySell, parse errors come mostly from a malformed trade line; and in Ultimatum, failures are usually due to a missing \texttt{<move>} tag.

% DOES OUR RETRY LOOP WORK? IF SO DOES IT CHANGE RESULTS?
The proposed self-correction loop (Section~\ref{sec:retry-ablation}) corrects most malformed moves. Of the 10\% of moves that triggered a self-correction loop, 75\% were fixed on the first retry and 93\% within the three-retry budget (Figure~\ref{fig:retried-moves-resolution}). Only 28 games still failed to complete, almost all of them (26 of 28) caused by \texttt{Gemma-3-4B-it}. Qwen triggered most of the retries (284 of 414) but almost always recovered.

    \centering

    \caption{Retry-3 recovery breakdown. Most corrected moves parse after one retry.}
    \label{fig:retried-moves-resolution}

The retry loop does not materially change the rankings. Comparing win rates under no retries and retry-3 gives a Spearman correlation $\rho$ of $0.92$, and the pooled win rate by family and tier preserves the relative ordering of families (Figure~\ref{fig:winrate-retry-ablation}, Appendix~\ref{ap3:additional-results}). Because the retry loop preserves the overall ranking while recovering lost games, the rest of the chapter focuses on the retry-3 results.

\subsection{Role and Turn} \label{sec:role-and-turn}

%DOES ROLE AND TURN MATTER THE SAME WAY THEY DID WITH CLOSE-SOURCE MODELS?
One of the conclusions reported by \textcite{bianchi_how_2024} was that turn and role matter, with the proposer (player~1) holding an advantage in the Multi-Turn Ultimatum, whereas in the Trading and BuySell scenarios the advantage shifts to the second player.

The same asymmetries appear with open-weight models. Figure~\ref{fig:cross-play-role-effect} plots each family's win rate in both seats, pooled across tiers. On average, being the second player increases the family's win rate by 47.7 percentage points in Trading and by 53.9 in BuySell. In the Ultimatum Game the gap is even wider and flips direction, with player~1 winning, on average, 81.5 percentage points more often as a proposer than as responder.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/results/cross_play/role_effect_family_seat.png}
    \caption{Win rate by family and seat under retry-3. Ultimatum favors the first mover, while Trading and BuySell favor the second mover.}
    \label{fig:cross-play-role-effect}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Game-Specific Findings}

%Previous subsection averaged over three very different games. Trading is zero-sum; BuySell is positive-sum with fixed surplus and Ultimatum is dominated by the proposer seat;

% This subsection analyzes one scenario at a time

The previous subsections averaged over three games with very different structures. Trading and BuySell both favour the second player, whereas the Ultimatum is dominated by the first; and where Trading is zero-sum, BuySell is positive-sum. This subsection analyzes the games one at a time. The per-pair win and payoff matrices for each game are provided in Appendix~\ref{ap3:additional-results} (Figures~\ref{fig:pairwise-trading} to \ref{fig:pairwise-buysell}).

\paragraph{Trading.}

Since both resources (X and Y) have the same value and each player starts with 30 units in total (25 of one resource, 5 of the other), every deal is zero-sum: a gain by one party is a loss of the same magnitude to the other. This may help explain the role effect in Figure~\ref{fig:cross-play-role-effect}. The second player has more information, evaluating an offer before making a counter-proposal, while the first mover must reveal what it is willing to give up.

Figure~\ref{fig:pairwise-trading} shows that \texttt{Qwen-3.5-27B} is the strongest model, and the only one to win from both seats. As player~2 it wins every matchup; as player~1 it beats \texttt{Gemma-3-27B-it} but splits evenly with \texttt{Mistral-Small-3.2-24B}. Gemma's weakness in the Trading game is also evident in its being the only model family with a negative mean Trading payoff in every tier (Table~\ref{tab:cross-play-family-summary}).

\paragraph{Multi-Turn Ultimatum.}

Section~\ref{sec:role-and-turn} showed that in Ultimatum the role matters more than in any other game, with the proposer winning 85--97\% of decisive games. Figure~\ref{fig:ultimatum-opening-offer} shows that proposers usually open well below an even split, with families diverging in their aggressiveness. Mistral is the most generous proposer, particularly at the very-small and medium tiers, where it offers \$40.9 and \$45.8 respectively and keeps roughly half of the pot. Qwen and Gemma are far more aggressive (\$7.7 for very-small Qwen and \$8.8 for small Gemma). In our case agression pays. At the medium tier for example, Gemma offers \$22.1 on average and keeps \$73.9 while Mistral offers \$45.8 and only keeps \$49.9.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/results/cross_play/ultimatum_opening_offer.png}
    \caption{Mean opening offer in Ultimatum, measured as dollars offered to the responder. Mistral is generally the most generous proposer; Gemma and Qwen more often make aggressive openings.}
    \label{fig:ultimatum-opening-offer}
\end{figure}

\textcite{bianchi_how_2024} noticed irrational behaviour in some proprietary models playing this variation of the Ultimatum game in which players are allowed to counter the proposals they receive. Yet in some circumstances some models reject offers outright without a counter-offer, which is a value-destroying move for both. We also noticed this irrational behaviors in open-weight models. These rejections are concentrated in the very-small tier (Figure~\ref{fig:ultimatum-rejection-rate}), where \texttt{Gemma-3-4B} rejects 32.2\% of the splits (Mistral 11.9\%, Qwen 0\%). Rejected games do start from more aggressive openings (a mean offer of \$18.9, against \$24.1 in accepted games), but rejection remains value-destroying in a situation where a counter-proposal would benefit both sides.

    \label{fig:ultimatum-rejection-rate}
\end{figure}


\paragraph{BuySell.}

% BuySell is positive sum: which both parties can profit. Seller cost 40, buyer value 60, so any price in between splits a 20-ZUP surplus.

BuySell is a positive-sum game in which any price between the seller's cost of production (40) and the buyer's willingness to pay (60) creates a positive payoff. The buyer captures most of the surplus, which leads to the second-player winrate advantage shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cross-play-role-effect}. The average deal gives the seller 5.7 ZUP and the buyer 14.3 ZUP (Figure~\ref{fig:buysell-price-surplus}).

\textcite{bianchi_how_2024} observed an anchoring effect in NegotiationArena, where the final accepted price correlates strongly with the initial proposed price. We find the same in open-weight models: Figure~\ref{fig:buysell-seller-anchoring} shows a 0.752 correlation between the seller's opening price and the final agreed price, close to the 0.716 they report. The difference in behavior between Qwen and Gemma illustrates this. Qwen sellers open at 48.5 on average and settle at 49.0, while Gemma sellers open much lower on average, at 40.2, and settle at 43.0.

\end{figure}

%Irrationality: Gemma-3-12B-it opens below its own 40 cost in 76.7% of games, 46.7% of deals close below cost; Qwen almost never does.

We also observe some irrational pricing. Across games, sellers open bellow their production cost in 16.2\% of games, and 12.9\% of completed deals close below cost. \texttt{Gemma-3-12B-it} is particularly irrational, opening bellow production cost in 76.7\% of games. Qwen on the other hand almost never make this error.
