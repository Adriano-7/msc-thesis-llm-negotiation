Negotiation is ubiquitous in life

\section{Motivation}

NegotiationArena \parencite{bianchi_how_2024} is an open-source framework that evaluates the negotiation abilities of \ac{LLM} agents across three scenarios (price negotiation, resource trading and a multi-turn ultimatum game) using win rate and payoff as metrics. It is a strong basis for studying negotiation, but its original results covered only proprietary models, some of which have since been deprecated. This raises concerns about reproducibility and accessibility of the results and leaves open the question of how the smaller, openly available models behave in the same scenarios.

Because negotiation is hard, and retraining large models is costly, a natural follow-up question is whether their outcomes can be improved at inference time, without changing the weights. Two ideas are particularly promising. The first is Self-Refine \parencite{madaan_self-refine_2023}, in which an agent critiques and rewrites its own answer before commiting to it, a technique shown to help on tasks such as mathematical and code reasoning. The second is multi-agent deliberation, in which several models propose, discuss and vote on a shared answer. Recent work reports quality gains from round-table \parencite{chen2024reconcileroundtableconferenceimproves} and debate \parencite{du2023improvingfactualityreasoninglanguage}, and earlier, pre-\ac{LLM} research found that team-based negotiation tend to outperform single agents \parencite{S_nchez_Anguix_2013}. Both techniques, however, multiply the cost so the central question is not only whether they help, but whether any gains justify the aditional cost.

\section{Research Questions}
This thesis is guided by the following research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{RQ1: How well do open-weight \acp{LLM} negotiate across the three NegotiationArena games and parameter tiers, and which behaviours reported for proprietary models, such as price anchoring, positional advantage, and occasional irrationality, carry over to them?}
    \item \textit{RQ2: Do the social-persona effects that raised GPT-4's win rate and payoff transfer to open-weight models?}
    \item \textit{RQ3: Does a draft\,$\rightarrow$\,critique\,$\rightarrow$\,rewrite (Self-Refine) loop improve an agent's negotiation outcomes enough to justify its additional inference cost?}
    \item \textit{RQ4: Does replacing a single negotiating party with a deliberating team of models improve outcomes over a single agent, and does team diversity (homogeneous versus heterogeneous) matter?}
\end{itemize}

To answer them, we keep the NegotiationArena games and protocol unchanged and replace its proprietary agents with open-weight models drawn from three families (Gemma, Mistral and Qwen) and grouped into three paramenter tiers. RQ1 is addressed by cross-playing these models against one another across the three games and tiers, and comparing the resulting behaviours with those reported for proprietary models.
