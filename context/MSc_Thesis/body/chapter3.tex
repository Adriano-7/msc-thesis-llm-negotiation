\section{NegotiationArena Framework} \label{sec:na-framework}

\textcite{bianchi_how_2024} introduced NegotiationArena, an open-source framework to evaluate and analyze the negotiation abilities of LLM agents. The original work focused on proprietary models, some of which have since been deprecated; we keep the games and protocol unchanged, but replace the agents with the open-weight models identified in Table~\ref{tab:experiment-models} and extend it by implementing two inference-time techniques (Sections~\ref{sec:self-refine} and~\ref{sec:team-negotiation}). 

\subsection{Games} \label{sec:games}
% Explicar: Ultimatum Game; Trading Game; Price Negotiation (BuySell)
% Possíveis ações; o que significa ganhar em cada um deles?; 

In each scenario, two agents alternate turns for up to a fixed number of rounds, or until one side terminates the game. Before each move, the agent reasons privately step by step; this reasoning is never revealed to the opponent.

\paragraph{Resource Exchange Scenario (Trading game).}
In the trading game, each party starts with a set of resources and aims to maximise their total holdings. The parties exchange proposals until one is accepted or the maximum number of rounds is reached. The agent with the greater gain wins.

\paragraph{Multi-Turn Ultimatum Game.}
In this variation of the classic Ultimatum Game \cite{doi:10.1126/science.1082976}, one agent receives all of the game's resources but must propose a split that the other accepts in order for either to keep any of it. The second agent can either accept, counter-propose a new split, or reject outright, causing both agents to lose all resources. If no agreement is reached within the maximum number of rounds, both agents lose everything. The winner is the agent with the higher share of the agreed split.

\paragraph{Seller and Buyer Scenario.}
This is an incomplete-information game in which a seller and a buyer negotiate the price of an item. Each party holds a private valuation: the seller knows its production cost and the buyer its willingness to pay. On each turn, a player may issue a \textsc{Proposal}, \textsc{Accept} the opponent's offer, or \textsc{Reject} and end the game with no deal. An agent wins if the agreed price is closer to the opponent's reservation value than to its own.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

% Usamos Win Rate (percentagem de jogos ganhos) e Average Payoff como usaram no NegotiationArena
% Para além disso analisamos a completion rate - percentagem de jogos que alcançam um estado final (por vezes não alcançam por um erro de protocolo/formato). Esta métrica é especialmente importante para medir o impacto do nosso mecanismo de retry 
Negotiation performance is measured using the two metrics adopted in the NegotiationArena (Win Rate and Average Payoff) complemented by a Completion Rate.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Win Rate:} Percentage of \textit{completed} games in which a party obtained a higher payoff than the other. As in NegotiationArena, ties are ignored.
    \item \textbf{Average Payoff:} Average number of resources of each agent after the trade. Its definition is game-specific: in Price Negotiation it is the surplus ($\text{price} - \text{cost}$ for the seller, $\text{value} - \text{price}$ for the buyer); in Trading it is the net change in resource value ($\text{final} - \text{initial}$), which may be negative; and in Ultimatum it is the number of dollars retained.
    \item \textbf{Completion Rate:} Proportion of games that reach a final state. Because smaller open-weight models frequently violated the response format, this metric is reported alongside the others, and is the primary metric for the retry ablation.
\end{itemize}

Open-weight models vary considerably in scale, so our objective was to group them into comparable parameter tiers in order to measure performance differences across model sizes. We defined three tiers: 4–9B, 12–14B, and 24–27B parameters. 

For each tier, we selected models from three families: Qwen, Mistral, and Gemma. We initially considered Llama models as well, but we noticed difficulties in adhering to the required output format; given the additional computational overhead this would entail, we excluded them from the benchmark. The selected models are listed in Table~\ref{tab:experiment-models}.

We applied $8$-bit quantization (\texttt{bitsandbytes}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/bitsandbytes-foundation/bitsandbytes}}) to Gemma, Qwen and Mistral-Small models; the Ministral models are already distributed in native FP8 precision. Generation uses temperature $0.7$ and top-p $0.9$ sampling, with a maximum of $1024$ new tokens per generation call.

The first contribution follows the experimental framework of NegotiationArena \cite{bianchi_how_2024}, evaluating open-weight models in place of the proprietary models of the original study. Our goal is to establish a baseline of how well current open-weight models negotiate across the three games and parameter tiers. Additionally, following \textcite{bianchi_how_2024}, we examine whether assigning a social persona changes the outcome of negotiations.

\subsection{Model Cross-Play} \label{sec:cross-play}

To measure how well current open-weight models negotiate among themselves, we run a round-robin within each parameter tier. Each ordered pairing is played for 30 games in each of the three scenarios, using the default parameters of Table~\ref{tab:game-setup}. Because the games are role-asymmetric, both orderings of each pair are executed. The analysis of win rate and average payoff over all opponents allows for a per-model ranking. 

\subsection{Social Personas} \label{sec:personas}

Following \textcite{bianchi_how_2024}, we analyse whether assigning a social persona to one party changes the outcomes of the negotiations. Player 1 always uses the default behaviour, while Player 2 is assigned one of three personas: \textit{baseline} (no persona), \textit{desperate} (instructed to feign desperation and beg for a better deal), or \textit{cunning} (instructed to be manipulative and to insult the opponent). The persona text is appended directly to Player~2's game system prompt; the exact wording for each persona and game is listed in Table~\ref{tab:persona-prompts}. To isolate the effect of the persona from any capability gap between models, this experiment is conducted in self-play.

\subsection{Self-Correction} \label{sec:retry-ablation} 

A practical obstacle with smaller open-weight models was that they frequently produced responses violating the required tag format, which could not be parsed into a valid move and ended the game prematurely. To address this, we test whether giving a model the opportunity to correct its formatting helps. When a response fails to parse, the agent receives an error message containing its offending response together with the parser error, and is asked to resubmit its move in the correct format; this repeats up to \texttt{max\_retries} times, after which the game ends unparsed and is counted as incomplete. Figure~\ref{fig:retry_loop} illustrates this loop. We compare the baseline (\texttt{max\_retries}\,$=0$) against \texttt{max\_retries}\,$=3$ across both experiments, using Completion Rate as the primary metric.

\section{Self-Refine} \label{sec:self-refine}

% Baseado no artigo \cite{madaan_self-refine_2023} 
% Citar prompts que estão no anexo;

We then investigate whether a model negotiates better when given the chance to critique and rewrite its own move before committing to it. As introduced in Section~\ref{sec:self-refinement}, we apply the Self-Refine algorithm of \textcite{madaan_self-refine_2023}: rather than immediately committing to an answer, the agent cycles through a \textit{draft $\rightarrow$ feedback $\rightarrow$ refine} loop, using its own critique as the only source of feedback. Figure~\ref{fig:self_refine_loop} illustrates the adapted loop.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures//methodology/SelfRefine___Figure.jpg}
    \caption{Self-Refine process. After generating an initial draft (Phase 1), the agent critiques its output along five axes (Phase 2). Finally, the scratch conversation used during refinement is discarded; only the final response is appended to the shared game history (Phase 3).}
    \label{fig:self_refine_loop}
\end{figure}

Whenever an agent must choose a move, it first produces an initial draft and then runs two critique-and-rewrite iterations. Each iteration prompts the agent to critique its current draft along the following negotiation-specific axes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Format:} whether every required tag is present and parseable;
\item \textbf{Payoff Alignment:} whether the proposed trade advances the agent's own valuation or goal;
\item \textbf{Opponent Plausibility:} whether the move is likely to be accepted given the dialogue so far;
\item \textbf{Consistency:} whether the reasoning, message, and move agree with one another;
\item \textbf{Rule Compliance:} whether the proposal budget and turn limits are respected.
\end{itemize}

A second \textit{refine} prompt then asks the model to rewrite the response, incorporating the flagged changes. The critique and intermediate drafts are produced on a scratch copy of the conversation; once refinement ends, this scratch copy is discarded and only the final response is appended to the shared game history.

We evaluate the four combinations obtained by assigning each party either the default or the Self-Refine strategy: both default, both Self-Refine, and the two mixed conditions. As in the social-persona experiment (Section~\ref{sec:personas}), every condition is run in self-play so that any difference is attributable to the strategy rather than to a capability gap between models; each condition is played for 30 games across all three scenarios and model tiers. The feedback and refinement prompts are given in Appendix~\ref{ap1:sr-prompts}.

Unlike the previous experiments, each move requires five model calls: one initial draft plus, for each of the two iterations, one feedback and one rewrite call (Table~\ref{tab:inference-cost}). Whether this increase in inference cost delivers a measurable improvement in negotiation performance is one of the main questions taken up in the results chapter.

\section{Team Negotiation} \label{sec:team-negotiation}

The final contribution replaces a single negotiation party with a \textit{team} of \textit{N} models that debate internally before emitting one move. Earlier (pre-LLM) research in automated multi-agent systems has shown that team-based negotiation strategies generally outperform those of single agents \cite{S_nchez_Anguix_2013}. In parallel, recent multi-agent work shows that letting several language models propose, critique and vote on a shared answer can raise quality above that of any single member, whether through round-table consensus \cite{chen2024reconcileroundtableconferenceimproves} or debate \cite{du2023improvingfactualityreasoninglanguage}. We bring these ideas together and ask whether a deliberating team of LLMs negotiates better than a single agent. 

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/methodology/team_based_negotiation_fig.png}
    \caption{Team deliberation process. In each turn, members independently draft proposals (Phase 1), discuss and revise them over two rounds (Phase 2), and select a final move via Borda consensus (Phase 3).}
    \label{fig:team_negotiation}
\end{figure}

We represent one party by a team of \textit{N} members and let them deliberate privately each turn before emitting a move. A turn is divided into three phases, \textit{draft $\rightarrow$ discuss $\rightarrow$ consensus}, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:team_negotiation}. 

In the \textbf{drafting} phase each member generates a possible move from the same prompt a single agent receives, so after the first phase the team ends with up to \textit{N} distinct proposals.

In the \textbf{discussion} phase every member is shown its own draft alongside its teammates' proposals. Each member then keeps, merges or adopts whichever proposal it judges strongest for the team and re-emits a full move. The discussion prompt is given in Appendix~\ref{ap1:team-prompts}.

Finally, in the \textbf{consensus} phase, the revised drafts form a slate of candidate moves and each member ranks the entire slate from best to worst for the team's payoff (Listing~\ref{lst:team-ranking}). A Borda count over these rankings selects the move: in a ranking of $s$ candidates, the candidate in the top position ($k=0$) earns $s-1-k = s-1$ points, the next $s-2$, and so on down to $0$ for the last. The move with the highest total wins, with a deterministic lowest-index tie-break. Unlike the Similarity-Based Borda Voting strategy of \textcite{S_nchez_Anguix_2013}, accepting the opponent's offer is not handled separately; it is simply one of the moves a member can draft, so a single vote decides both \textit{what} to propose and whether to \textit{accept}, with no need for a separate voting phase to determine acceptance.

The entire deliberation is private. The drafting, discussion, and ranking prompts are run in place of each member's own conversation, but once the team's move is chosen the history is rolled back to the initial snapshot, and only the agreed move is appended to each member's history. The opposition party therefore sees exactly one move per turn, with none of the internal back-and-forth. One complication follows from this rollback: the reasoning attached to the winning move was written in the context of the discussion and may refer to teammates that are no longer visible once the deliberation is erased. To keep each member's private history coherent on subsequent turns, before committing the move, the winning member rewrites its \texttt{<reason>} block into a first-person reasoning that mentions no teammates, candidates, ranking or ``team'', while keeping the same strategy and conclusion (Listing~\ref{lst:team-reason}).

The central axis of this experiment is team diversity. We compare \textbf{homogeneous} teams, made of \textit{N} copies of a single model, against \textbf{heterogeneous} teams whose members are drawn from different model families. We fix the team size at $N = 3$ and the number of discussion rounds at $R = 2$. For this experiment we only analysed the models in the small category (12--14B). The heterogeneous team is composed of one model from each family.  

A homogeneous team plays only against a single agent of its own model: a team of three Gemmas faces a lone Gemma, a team of Qwens a lone Qwen, and so on. The heterogeneous team matches against each of the three families in turn.

As with Self-Refine, this deliberation comes with extra costs. A turn costs $N$ drafting calls, $N \times R$ revision calls during discussion, $N$ ranking calls, and one reasoning-rewrite call, for a total of $N(2+R)+1$ calls. In our experiments, $N = 3$ and $R = 2$, so there is a thirteen-fold increase in the number of calls compared with the default (Table~\ref{tab:inference-cost}).
